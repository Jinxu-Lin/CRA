# Soundness Critique Report

## Overall Assessment
- **Score**: 7.0 / 10
- **Core Assessment**: The causal reasoning chain (Gap -> Three Bottlenecks -> Repair Mechanisms -> 2x2 Validation) is well-constructed and internally consistent. The factorial ablation design is methodologically sound for isolating main effects and interactions. The main soundness concerns are: (1) the FM1 argument relies heavily on the Johnson-Lindenstrauss analogy, which applies to *random* vectors but gradients are not random; (2) the paper conflates "representation similarity" with "influence" without adequately addressing the correlation-vs-causation gap; and (3) the independence argument for the three bottlenecks lacks formal justification.
- **Would this dimension cause reject at a top venue?**: Unlikely as sole cause, but the FM1 theoretical argument has a real gap that a theory-oriented reviewer would identify.

## Issues (by severity)

### [Critical] FM1 argument via JL concentration assumes gradients are approximately random, but they are not
- **Location**: Section 3.2, Bottleneck 2 (FM1)
- **Problem**: The paper states: "By the Johnson-Lindenstrauss concentration phenomenon, inner products of random vectors in R^B concentrate around zero when B >> log^2(N). At B ~ 10^9 and N ~ 10^4, the ratio B/log^2(N) ~ 6 x 10^6, placing per-sample gradients deep in the approximately-orthogonal regime." However, JL concentration applies to *random* vectors. Per-sample gradients are NOT random -- they are structured by the loss landscape, share the same model architecture, and are computed on potentially similar data points. Gradients of similar training samples (e.g., two toxic sentences) will have correlated gradients precisely because they affect the model similarly. The entire FM1 argument assumes gradient inner products are dominated by noise, but the relevant inner products (between influential training samples and the test sample) may be in the structured, non-random subspace.
- **Simulated reviewer phrasing**: "The JL-based argument for FM1 is fundamentally flawed. JL applies to random projections of fixed vectors, not to structured objects like neural network gradients. The gradients of two semantically similar samples will have correlated components precisely in the subspace relevant for influence computation. The authors need to provide evidence that per-sample gradient inner products actually concentrate around zero at LLM scale, rather than arguing by analogy to random vectors."
- **Suggested fix**: (1) Replace or supplement the JL argument with empirical evidence: compute the distribution of gradient inner products at Pythia-1B scale and show they indeed concentrate. (2) Cite Li et al.'s iHVP degeneracy analysis more prominently as the *primary* evidence for FM1, since it is a direct empirical observation rather than an analogy. (3) Acknowledge that FM1 may be a spectrum -- not all gradient inner products are equally diluted, and the dilution may be worse for dissimilar samples (where influence is low anyway).

### [Major] Correlation vs. causation gap in representation-space TDA is acknowledged but not resolved
- **Location**: Section 3.3 (FM1 Repair), Section 4.2 (Experiment 1 analysis)
- **Problem**: The paper acknowledges: "RepSim captures representational similarity (correlational), not counterfactual influence (causal). The gap between P@K and LDS will directly test this assumption." This is a fundamental issue: if representation similarity does not predict counterfactual influence (LDS), then the entire FM1 "repair" is not actually repairing the influence computation -- it is replacing it with a different, possibly unrelated metric. The paper's primary metric is LDS (counterfactual), and if RepSim's P@K is high but LDS is low, the paper must fundamentally reconsider whether moving to representation space is a "repair" or a "replacement."
- **Simulated reviewer phrasing**: "The authors claim representation-space attribution 'fixes' FM1, but representation similarity and influence are conceptually different. A sample can be highly similar in representation space without being influential (and vice versa). The 2x2 ablation assumes that LDS improvements from RepSim are due to 'fixing FM1,' but they could simply reflect that cosine similarity in representation space is a reasonable heuristic for a different quantity than influence."
- **Suggested fix**: Be more precise about what "FM1 repair" means. Distinguish between two interpretations: (a) representation-space methods genuinely compute influence better by concentrating signal, or (b) representation-space methods compute a *different* quantity (similarity) that happens to correlate with downstream metrics. The factorial ablation cannot distinguish these. At minimum, add a paragraph in S3.3 or S5 discussing this distinction.

### [Major] Independence argument for three bottlenecks is informal
- **Location**: Section 3.2, "Independence argument" paragraph
- **Problem**: The paper states: "The three bottlenecks arise from fundamentally different mechanisms: Hessian error is a computational approximation problem, FM1 is an inherent dimensionality problem, and FM2 is a scoring bias problem independent of both space and Hessian quality. This structural distinctness predicts that they should be largely independent." This is an assertion, not an argument. The claim that "different mechanisms" implies "independence" does not follow logically. For example: Hessian error could be *worse* in high-dimensional space (coupling Hessian and FM1); representation space could partially debias common-mode signals (coupling FM1 and FM2). The paper tests independence empirically (via interaction term), but the *prediction* of independence needs a stronger basis.
- **Suggested fix**: (1) Acknowledge that independence is an empirical hypothesis, not a theoretical guarantee. Rephrase: "We hypothesize that the three bottlenecks are approximately independent because they arise from different mechanisms. We test this hypothesis through the factorial ablation." (2) Discuss specific mechanisms by which independence might fail, e.g., "If representation space inherently captures task-specific features (partially addressing FM2), the interaction term would be negative (subadditive)."

### [Minor] The 30% interaction threshold is arbitrary
- **Location**: Section 3.4
- **Problem**: The paper adopts "|Xi| < 30% of min(|Delta_FM1|, |Delta_FM2|)" as the criterion for "clean independence." This threshold is not motivated by any statistical or theoretical principle. Why 30% and not 20% or 50%?
- **Suggested fix**: Either justify the 30% threshold (e.g., by reference to standard ANOVA effect-size conventions) or present it as a descriptive guideline rather than a formal criterion. Report the actual interaction magnitude and let readers judge.

### [Minor] CMRR definition is not standard and may be misleading
- **Location**: Section 3.4, Eq. for CMRR
- **Problem**: CMRR = |I_std - I_contr| / |I_std| is defined as a ratio over absolute standard scores. If I_std is close to zero for some samples (as expected if attribution is noisy), CMRR becomes numerically unstable. Additionally, "CMRR" borrows from electronics (common-mode rejection ratio) but the definition differs from the standard engineering definition (which is a ratio of differential gain to common-mode gain). This may confuse readers from signal-processing backgrounds.
- **Suggested fix**: Rename to "Contrastive Correction Fraction" or similar, avoiding the loaded "CMRR" term. Add a note about numerical stability for near-zero denominators.

### [Minor] Contrastive scoring assumes theta_base is a meaningful reference
- **Location**: Section 3.3 (FM2 Repair)
- **Problem**: The paper acknowledges this partially: "The subtraction is particularly natural for tasks where the base model has no task-specific knowledge (e.g., toxicity filtering), and less natural for tasks where the base model already possesses relevant knowledge (e.g., factual attribution)." However, for factual attribution, the base model (e.g., Pythia-1B pre-trained) may already encode many entity-fact associations. Subtracting base-model scores could remove genuine influence signal, not just noise. This is not just "less natural" -- it could actively hurt performance.
- **Suggested fix**: Predict and discuss the possibility that contrastive scoring may *degrade* LDS on factual attribution, where the base model already has relevant knowledge. Present this as a testable prediction of the framework.

## Strengths
- The factorial ablation design (2x2) is methodologically rigorous and well-suited for decomposing main effects and interactions. The statistical analysis plan (permutation tests, bootstrap CI, FDR correction) is appropriate.
- The paper is explicit about chain-validity conditions: "if RepSim fails on LDS, FM1 thesis has no LDS-level support." This intellectual honesty strengthens credibility.
- The framing of MAGIC as a diagnostic upper bound rather than a proposed method is sound and avoids over-claiming.

## Summary Recommendations
The core reasoning chain is solid and the experimental design is appropriate for the claims. The two main issues requiring attention are: (1) the FM1 argument via JL concentration needs empirical grounding rather than analogy to random vectors, and (2) the correlation-vs-causation gap between representation similarity and influence needs explicit discussion, not just acknowledgment. These are fixable through better framing and additional analysis (e.g., gradient inner product distribution, explicit discussion of what "repair" means). The independence argument should be reframed as an empirical hypothesis rather than a prediction from "structural distinctness."
